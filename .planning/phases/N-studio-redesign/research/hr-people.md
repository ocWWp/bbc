# Persona research — HR / People Ops (2026)

> Deep-research agent output, 2026 sources. New role (not in the original 5).

## Summary
"People ops" at a 5-50 person startup is rarely a department — it's the founder doing HR off the side of the desk (5-20 ppl), an ops generalist who absorbs it (~20-40), or a first "Head of People" generalist (~40-80, Series A trigger). They're document-producers under constant context-switching. The writing they ship is **high-stakes, legally sensitive, judged on specificity and fairness.**

## What they produce
- **Job descriptions** — scope, leveling, must-haves vs nice-to-haves.
- **Offer letters & employment contracts** — comp, equity, start terms.
- **Onboarding docs/checklists** — pre-boarding, week 1, 30-60-90, split across HR/manager/IT/buddy lanes.
- **Performance review frameworks + individual write-ups** — behavior-based, tied to the JD, balanced (≥1 real strength + ≥1 growth area).
- **Company values / culture guide** — strategic doc, distinct from the handbook.
- **People policies** — PTO, remote/hybrid, conduct, security.
- **Comp band documentation** — min/midpoint/max per level, philosophy, percentile target.
- **Internal announcements, 1:1 frameworks, PIP/exit communications.**

## Workflow
Gather context first: existing/comparable roles, current comp bands, the values doc, prior versions of the same doc, leveling rubric, the hiring manager's input. On screen: a blank doc beside 4-6 reference tabs (old JD, comp sheet, ATS, Slack thread). Draft → route for review → revise.

## Good vs. mediocre
**Good:** specific and behavior-based ("three Q3 reports were late and missing sections"), forward-looking, tied to the JD, balanced; reviews are a *summary of conversations already had*, never a surprise. **Mediocre:** vague personality judgments, all-positive reviews, boilerplate JDs, ambushes. "Done" = specific, fair, legally defensible, on-voice, reviewed (by hiring manager / founder / sometimes counsel).

## Workspace preferences
On-screen: the relevant prior doc, comp bands, values/voice, leveling rubric — without hunting. Hate: context-switching, Google Drive as "where files go to disappear," scattered docs and tribal knowledge, organizing more than writing.

## Design implications for the People Studio
- Draft **with the company's actual context** — values, comp bands, prior docs, voice — as always-loaded grounding.
- **Bias / sensitivity flagging** is a first-class need — AI mirrors trained-in bias (recruiters echoed biased AI up to 90% in a 2025 study); disadvantages neurodiverse candidates. The Studio should flag, keep human-in-loop, "structured data that informs human decisions, not autonomous decisions."
- Behavior-based scaffolding for review write-ups (the specificity bar).
- Output is legally-sensitive — for PIP/exit/termination, prompt "send this to counsel" (overlaps with the Legal Studio's triage pattern).
